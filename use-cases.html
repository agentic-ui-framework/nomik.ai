Use cases

See your business in it.

The pattern is always the same: you state intent, the fleet does the work in the open, triggers pull in recurring work, and you approve only the consequential calls. Here's a day in the life.

E-commerce · 6 people · ~$4M/yr on Shopify

"Lumio", DTC home goods

Drowning in support tickets, ad busywork, and manual bookkeeping, no time to grow because they're running to stand still.

Squads: Growth · Support · Operations · the Warden

StackKG
  • Overnight: a payment_failed → dunning trigger recovers carts; Support drafts every ticket, sends the routine ones, holds refunds for approval.
  • A call comes in: a voice agent answers, looks up the order, offers store credit, escalates the angry one with a transcript ready.
  • "Plan a spring sale": Growth drafts the campaign and budget; the founder approves the spend and they launch.

What changed: support runs around the clock; the founder spends the day on growth, not the inbox.

SaaS · seed-stage B2B · 12 people

"Cadence", the most grounded scenario

Small eng team, a backlog that never shrinks, support and sales falling on the founders.

Squads: Engineering · Product · Growth · Support

StackS
  • A goal in chat: the orchestrator triages, the bug routes to an engineer, the feature goes to a principal for a spec.
  • It ships: the engineer patches and tests green inline; QA verifies; the founder approves the deploy in one click.
  • Signals flow in: triggers turn product feedback into tasks and enterprise leads into ready-to-send outreach.

What changed: the founders set direction and approved a handful of things. The team did the rest.

Agency · 15 people · 9 client retainers

"Field & Co", creative + marketing

The bottleneck is coordination, every client wants updates, every project runs in parallel, and senior people drown in status admin.

Squads: one workspace per client, an account lead (human) + Growth/Content/Design agents + a per-workspace Warden

StackSH
  • Per client: each workspace's Warden posts a status digest, and agents draft posts and creatives the lead approves in threads.
  • A request lands: an inbound email → scope + task trigger turns "can we add a landing page?" into an estimate the lead sends.
  • Month-end: a trigger has timesheets and invoices ready; the owner reviews margins per client.

What changed: more clients, no more coordination overhead. Seniors do senior work; agents do the glue.

Regulated · 40-person EU fintech · the on-prem wedge

"Severn", sovereignty-sensitive

Wants an agent workforce but cannot send customer data, code, or secrets to third-party model providers. Most agent platforms are a non-starter.

Why Nomik fits: deployed on-prem in Severn's own data center, with agents on local, self-hosted models, nothing leaves the building.

Stack (in your boundary)P
  • Identical collaboration to the SaaS scenario, squads, board, inline traces, approvals, but every model call hits a local model on Severn's GPUs.
  • A failed-payment → dunning and month-end → reconcile to DATEV run against EU-resident systems under GDPR controls.
  • Compliance reviews the glass-box audit (#events + decisions ledger) to confirm exactly what every agent did.

What changed: a regulated business gets the full leverage of an AI workforce without a data-exit risk, the capability competitors can't offer.
